-------------------------------------------
1. Morphology: The Structure of Words
-------------------------------------------
- Morphology is the study of how words are formed from smaller meaningful units called morphemes.
- The Focus: Internal word structure (prefixes, suffixes, roots).
- NLP Task: Stemming or Lemmatization. For example, an NLP model needs to know that "running," "runs," and "ran" all share the same root morphology ("run").
- Example: In the word unhappiness, the morphemes are un- (not), happy (root), and -ness (state of being).
-------------------------------------------
2. Syntax: The Structure of Sentences
-------------------------------------------
- Syntax is the "grammar" layer. It dictates the rules for how words are arranged to create grammatically correct sentences.
- The Focus: Word order, punctuation, and hierarchical structure.
- NLP Task: Dependency Parsing or Part-of-Speech (POS) Tagging.
- Example: "The cat chased the mouse" is syntactically correct. "Mouse the chased cat the" is not, even though the words are the same.
-------------------------------------------
3. Semantics: The Literal Meaning
-------------------------------------------
- Semantics moves beyond the rules and asks: "What do these words actually mean?" It looks at the literal definition of the words combined.
- The Focus: Meaning of words and their relationships (synonyms, antonyms, etc.).
- NLP Task: Word Embeddings (Word2Vec) or Named Entity Recognition (NER).
- Example: If I say "The emerald is green," semantics allows the AI to understand that an emerald is a gemstone and green is a color. It doesn't care about the context yet—just the definitions.
-------------------------------------------
4. Pragmatics: The Contextual Meaning
-------------------------------------------
- Pragmatics is the most complex layer. It looks at how context, social cues, and intent change the meaning of a sentence. This is where "reading between the lines" happens.
- The Focus: Intended meaning vs. literal meaning.
- NLP Task: Sentiment Analysis, Sarcasm Detection, or Dialogue Management.
- Example: If you ask a friend, "Do you have a watch?" and they answer "Yes" without telling you the time, they have understood the semantics but failed at the pragmatics. The pragmatic intent was: "Tell me what time it is."

1. Bag-of-Words (BoW)
------------------------
- This is the simplest approach. It literally throws all the words in a document into a "bag,"
- ignores their order (Syntax) and context (Pragmatics), and just counts how many times each word appears.
- The Logic: If two documents use the same words frequently, they are probably about the same topic.
- The Problem: It gives too much importance to common words like "the," "is," and "and" (Stop words), which don't actually tell you anything about the topic
- Example:
   - Doc A: "The cat sat."{the: 1, cat: 1, sat: 1}
   - Doc B: "The cat chased the mouse."{the: 2, cat: 1, chased: 1, mouse: 1}
2. TF-IDF (Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency)
------------------------
- TF-IDF is an "intelligent" version of Bag-of-Words. It scales the word counts based on how unique a word is to a specific document.
- It consists of two parts:
    - TF (Term Frequency): How often does the word appear in this document? (More is better).
    - IDF (Inverse Document Frequency): How rare is this word across all documents in your collection? (Rarer is better)

The "Modern" Gap
---------------
It's worth noting that both BoW and TF-IDF treat words as "atoms"—they don't understand that "King" and "Queen" are related. 
To solve that, we usually move from these frequency-based models to Word Embeddings (like Word2Vec) or Transformers (like BERT).
